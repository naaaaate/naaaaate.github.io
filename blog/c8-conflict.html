Conflict

Break it down

4/13/15

This week, we are writing about conflicts and breaking them down to the root of what the problem really is. A lot of times its really easy to say you feel angry but what's causing that feeling may be a deeper issue. The only way to understand that is to talk it out and break it down to its most basic form. Lets take a look at an example of conflict.
I think the last time I had a real conflict was with a family member over something as dumb as being on time to a basketball game. I told this family member I was going to be at their place around 7pm to pick them up for the game. I was running a little late because I was tired from all the other errands I had run that day. So when I texted this person that I was running a little late things escalated and we got into a huge argument. They wanted to be on time because they were looking forward to really going to this event. I didn't think it was a big deal to be on time because the seats were terrible, and I had been to so many games already knowing what to expect. I think the source of the conflict was that I didnt consider the other person's feelings, excitement, or how important this event was to them. On the other side I felt disrespected because of how tired I was from all the things I had to that day, and on top of that, they weren't even my tickets. They invited me to the game and I felt like I was being generous offering to drive even though I really didnt want to go.
The basic emotion I felt was anger and disrespect. I felt like the other person did not respect the fact that even though I didnt want to go to the game, or drive, and was tired from all the errands I had to run earlier that day, I still went out of the way to accomodate them, even if I was a late.
The actions I took to resolve the conflict was to stop texting, pull over to a gas station, and call the person on the phone. I heard their side of the story and they heard mine. I think if I could go back and change anything, I would have been more open and honest about how I felt and not gone to the event. The thing I learned most about this was to be more honest about my feelings even if its hard for the other person, or not what the other person wants to hear.


Thanks for reading,
~Nate
